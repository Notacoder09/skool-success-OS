# Creator Wisdom → Skool Success OS Product Decisions

**Source material:** Sam Ovens + Andrew Kirby Skoolers course (full),
Skool Games onboarding call, Hormozi business design training, Hamza
operator perspective video.

**Purpose:** Ground every product decision, default, threshold, and
piece of AI copy in actual creator-tested wisdom — not Claude’s general
knowledge.

## Part 1 — Extracted Wisdom (organized by category)

### A. Hard data points (these become our thresholds)

| Metric                                       | Value          | Source              |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------- | ------------------- |
| Platform-wide avg monthly retention          | 80%            | Skool internal data |
| “Good” retention                             | 90%+           | Skool benchmarks    |
| “Bad” retention                              | 70% or less    | Skool benchmarks    |
| Month 1, 2, 3 churn                          | ~20% each      | Skool data          |
| Month 6+ churn                               | ~2%            | Skool data          |
| Avg About page conversion (free)             | 20%            | Skool data          |
| Avg About page conversion (paid)             | 4%             | Skool data          |
| Avg paid community price                     | ~$50/mo        | Skool data          |
| Members from Skool discovery (avg)           | 22%            | Skool data          |
| Members from Skool discovery (top groups)    | 50%+           | Skool data          |
| Annual discount standard                     | 17%            | Skool/SaaS norm     |
| % of paid Skool communities that earn money  | 54.1%          | Hormozi/Skool       |
| Average paid community owner earnings        | $1,360/mo      | Hormozi/Skool       |

**Single most important pattern:** Time is the dominant churn variable.
Months 1–3 are always ~20% churn regardless of what creator does. After
day 90, churn drops to ~10%. After month 6, it drops to ~2%. **Don’t
react to month-1 churn data.**

### B. The most-repeated tactics (across all 4 sources)

These showed up in every transcript. They’re the base layer.

1. **Show up daily and actually care.** Sam’s morning sweep, Hamza’s
   daily phone post, Kirby’s “minimum viable presence.” Nick Sarrive
   ($290k/mo) — his “secret” was just this.
2. **Read more than you post (9:1 ratio).** Most important owner action
   is reading, not broadcasting.
3. **Pin good comments and posts.** Two-click action that signals
   “this is what good looks like” — community modeling follows.
4. **Build relationships one-on-one.** DM → “How’s [group] going for
   you?” → Zoom → meet in person. Sam’s exact escalation ladder.
5. **Less content beats more content.** AI Jack: 30% → 5.2% churn by
   deleting. Schoolers reduced from many posts/week to one. Members
   aren’t full-time in your group; you are.
6. **The longer they stay the less they churn.** Time is the lever. Day
   90 is the inflection point.

### C. Onboarding & first-time experience

- First action when joining a new community: invite 3 people from
  contacts (doubles success rate per Skool data)
- Pinned welcome post template: “Hey, I’m from [location]. For fun, I
  like to [X]. Here’s a pic of my workspace.” Photo is the key —
  people spot vibes and DM each other.
- Schedule weekly call immediately (“coffee hour” no agenda)
- About page only needs: short description, one image, “imagine a place
  where…” opener, screen recording of inside
- Founding members framing → bought-in early when things aren’t finished
- Quick win in first session = retention boost
- One creator wasted $27k and 6 months building a course nobody wanted
  because he built before listening

### D. Engagement & owner behavior

- Owner’s daily chores order: notifications → DMs → reported content →
  membership requests → read new posts → like good ones → delete bad
  ones → join conversations → pin great comments → 1:1 relationship
  building
- Sam’s exact words: “Every morning I sweep the whole community”
- Don’t auto-reply instantly to every post — leaves no space for other
  members
- Praise publicly (pin/like/comment), correct privately (DM)
- “Why?” or 🤔 or “congrats” works fine — don’t have to write essays
- Cake mix story (members need to “add an egg” to feel involved):
  always ask questions members can participate in
- Push first vs pull first: leave silence so members pull from you
  instead of forcing content on them

### E. Retention / anti-churn (the meat of our product)

**Diagnostic patterns**

- Don’t react to month-1 churn — always ~20%, not your fault
- Wait until group is 90+ days old before judging baseline
- Average drops naturally as group ages because more members hit month 6

**When members cancel**

- Skool gives you time before membership ends — message them
- Ask: “Why did you cancel?” Look for patterns across cancellations
- Ask best/longest members why they STAYED. Double down on those reasons

**Common churn patterns and fixes**

- Price → add lower tier (Evelyn $99 → $33; Skate IQ $29 → $9)
- Overwhelm → delete content, simplify, fewer posts/calls
- Not feeling part of it → onboarding outreach, intro post template,
  weekly call
- Couldn’t keep up → design for “30 min/week” person, not just hardcore
- No relationship with creator → DM ladder (Sam’s escalation)

**Structural retention boosters**

- Annual option with 17% discount → fewer renewal decisions = less
  churn
- Grandfather pricing when raising rates → cancelling means losing rate
- In-person meetups → strongest single retention force (nothing else
  creates that stability signal)
- Members feel “belonging” only when they can keep up

### F. Community culture / model citizens

- “Model citizen” = the member you want everyone to imitate. Identify
  them explicitly.
- How to elevate them: pin their posts, feature in content, DM them,
  Zoom, partner eventually
- Making members famous = community runs without you = retention spikes
- Inside jokes and memes can’t be forced; recognize and amplify when
  they emerge
- Diverse community > homogenous (one plant garden = one pest wipes
  everything)
- Rituals (weekly calls, themes, challenges) create culture
- 30-day challenges create highest engagement spikes

### G. What goes inside a paid community (Hormozi’s 3 buckets)

1. **Access** — to you, to guest experts, to other members
2. **Content** — exclusive courses, trainings
3. **Events** — virtual workshops, in-person meetups, cohorts,
   challenges

**Hormozi’s “skeleton key” insight:** with 500–5000 members, you can
get high-status experts to do Q&As because you offer distribution.
Charlie Morgan ($1M/yr) does calls in Hamza’s community — members
couldn’t access him solo.

**Value equation** — every offering should:

- Lower risk
- Reduce time to result
- Reduce effort/sacrifice

### H. Hamza’s specific call structures (from his operator perspective)

- **Coaching call:** All muted, brings ONE person on stage, 10-min
  timer, 1:1 advice. Members get something they could never afford
  solo.
- **Lecture:** 1 hour, screen share, draws diagrams. Exclusive content
  not on YouTube.
- **Special calls:** Brings in millionaire friends, doctors,
  specialists.
- **In-person meetups:** $1k lunch for 15 members. Math works because
  community revenue dwarfs cost. Members trade contacts with EACH
  OTHER, not just creator.

**Hamza’s daily ritual:** one casual post on phone, “few paragraphs,
motivating the boys.”

### I. Why members actually pay (Hamza’s core insight)

> “It was never about the information. Everything I say is out there
> for free. People join because of YOU.”

- Creators sell themselves, not info
- Members join because the creator’s brain “relates to them”
- Resonant fans convert, others scroll — built-in filter
- **Implication for our product:** we’re not helping creators package
  info, we’re helping creators **be more present with the people who
  already chose them**

### J. Moderation / bad actors

- Pure spam → ban + delete last 7 days (one click)
- Rule break, well-intentioned → delete WITH FEEDBACK (Skool emails
  them rule + post copy)
- Second offense → delete + DM
- Third offense → ban
- Better than permanent ban: 30-day suspension. Returning members
  often become top contributors.
- Moderators: pull from leaderboard top users. Give them mod role
  (delete/approve/ban, not billing/email).
- Write rules AFTER moderating for a while — categories emerge
  organically.

### K. Growth / traffic (less relevant to our product but worth knowing)

- Traffic × Conversion = Growth. Usually traffic is the bottleneck not
  conversion.
- Pattern across platforms: link in bio + native mention + consistency
- Skool discovery rewards engagement and good About page
- “Rule of 300” upgrade of Hormozi’s Rule of 100: 100min commenting +
  100min outreach + 100min content per day
- Affiliates: 20–40% commission standard. Top group ($360k/mo) gets
  50%+ from affiliates.

### L. Pricing wisdom

- Start free if no audience. Free → learn → upgrade to paid.
- Models: Free → Subscription → Premium (free + paid tier) → Tiers →
  One-time
- Premium model preserves 20% free conversion AND adds revenue
- Lower price ≠ always better (luxury can invert)
- Starting low and raising over time → grandfathered members reduce
  churn

## Part 2 — Mapping to Skool Success OS V1 Features

For each of our 5 features, what creator wisdom drives the defaults,
copy, and thresholds.

### Feature 1: Drop-Off Map with AI Insights

**What creator wisdom tells us**

- Members can’t keep up = they leave (overwhelm pattern)
- The longer they stay, the less they churn — getting them past day 90
  is the win
- Lecture-style content fails; participation-based content retains

**Defaults & thresholds**

- Flag any lesson with > 40% drop-off as “high attention” (members
  can’t keep up here)
- Highlight lessons in module 1–2 most aggressively (early drop = will
  never come back)
- Don’t flag drop-off in optional/bonus material

**AI insight copy direction**

- DON’T say: “X% of members didn’t complete this lesson”
- DO say: “When members stop here, they usually don’t come back. This
  is your day-90 problem. Fixing this lesson is worth more than adding
  a new one.”
- Reference the “less is more” principle: “Before adding new content,
  ask: would deleting half this lesson get more people through?”

**Why this works:** Mirrors AI Jack’s 30%→5.2% churn discovery.
Creators reflexively want to add content; we tell them to subtract.

### Feature 2: Student Flashcards (email-delivered)

**What creator wisdom tells us**

- Members aren’t full-time in your group; design for “30 min/week”
  person
- Quick wins early = retention boost
- Belonging = ability to keep up
- Email touch = staying in front of busy members
- Digital challenges hit 40–60% completion vs 5–15% for self-paced
  courses

**Hard constraint:** Skool API does not provide video transcripts.
Content sourcing is a layered decision tree.

**Content sourcing decision tree** (run in order, stop at first match):

1. Lesson description > 100 words → use it. **FREE.**
2. Lesson has PDF/doc attachments → extract text, combine with
   description. **FREE.**
3. Cached transcript exists in DB → use it. **FREE.**
4. Creator has transcription disabled in settings → SKIP, show notice.
5. Creator has hit monthly transcription quota → SKIP, show notice.
6. Creator opted in AND has quota → run Whisper, cache result, use
   transcript. **PAID.**

We also include lesson **title + free metadata + thumbnail context** in
the prompt at every step so even short descriptions can produce useful
cards. If combined text is too thin and the creator hasn’t opted into
Whisper, we skip with a clear reason rather than producing junk.

**Defaults & thresholds**

- Default ON for flashcard generation overall
- Default OFF for video transcription specifically (creator opts in
  once they understand the tradeoff)
- Send flashcards within 24–48 hours of lesson completion (peak
  recall, not 7 days later)
- 3–5 flashcards per lesson (not 10–20 — overwhelm rule)
- Skip lessons with < 100 words of usable content even after all
  sourcing attempts
- Transcripts cached forever; never re-transcribe a lesson unless
  creator updates it
- Transcription quota resets monthly; cache re-use is FREE

**Copy direction in welcome flow to creators**

- “Your members aren’t full-time in your community. They have jobs and
  families. Flashcards land in their inbox and keep your lessons alive
  in their week — without them having to log in. This is how you fight
  ‘couldn’t keep up’ churn.”

**Onboarding transparency (after we scan their courses)**

- “We’ve scanned your N lessons. M can generate flashcards immediately
  from text and PDFs you’ve already added. K are video-only — toggle
  on auto-transcription to include those, or skip and the system still
  works for the M we have.”

**Honest framing in disable-warning modal**

- “Are you sure? Your member-side data shows email touchpoints reduce
  90-day churn. We can show you the numbers if you want.”

**Per-lesson source visibility (in flashcards UI)**

- Each lesson shows where its flashcards came from: “description,”
  “PDF,” “cached transcript,” “transcribed (Whisper),” or “skipped —
  [reason]”
- Never silently transcribe; never silently skip. Creator always knows
  what’s happening.

**Member email tone (the actual student-facing email)**

- Neutral teacher voice (per Xavier’s direction)
- Uses member’s first name in subject + body
- Subject example: “{First name} — notes for Lesson 2, 60-second
  review”
- Body keeps it short: card → answer prompt → next card. No metrics
  about their progress, no upsell, no community plugs.

### Feature 3: Community Pulse Dashboard

**What creator wisdom tells us**

- The owner’s job is to “feel the community” — not stare at metrics
- Daily presence is the #1 tactic
- 10 true regulars matter more than 1000 strangers
- Activity patterns matter more than raw numbers
- “Likes” placeholder — Sam talks about likes constantly as the
  lightweight engagement signal

**Defaults & thresholds**

- Default landing view: “What’s the temperature of your community
  this week?” — qualitative read first, numbers second
- Surface model citizens (most-engaged, longest-tenure) prominently —
  this is who to DM today
- Show activity AS A PATTERN (am I trending up, flat, or down vs my
  own baseline) NOT as a leaderboard against other communities
- Day-of-week activity heatmap (when are members on?) → tells creator
  when to schedule calls/posts

**Copy direction**

- Replace “Active Members” with “Regulars This Week” (Skool-native
  language)
- Replace “Engagement Rate” with “Posts that landed” (creator-native)
- Add a “Who to DM today” widget with 3–5 names — this is the
  operator action, not just info

**v1 scope reality**

- Posts/likes tiles ship as “coming soon” in v1 (matches V2 mockup).
  No feed scraping until later.

**What we explicitly DON’T do**

- No vanity metrics that don’t drive action
- No comparison to other communities (would create
  platform-dependency anxiety, opposite of what creators want)
- No notification spam — creator should open dashboard, not get pinged

### Feature 4: Member Check-ins (At-Risk)

**What creator wisdom tells us**

- Sam’s escalation ladder: like → comment → DM → “sup” → “Zoom?” →
  meet in person
- Most important DM template: “How’s [group] going for you?”
- Catching members BEFORE they cancel beats winback emails
- Time is the lever — if member is sliding toward inactive, intervene
  by day 30 not day 75
- Members who feel personally seen by the creator stay
- The “sup” check-in works because it’s not pushy

**Defaults & thresholds**

- Flag a member as “at-risk” when:
  - Activity drops 50%+ vs their own 30-day baseline (not absolute
    thresholds — relative to themselves)
  - No posts/comments/likes in 14 days when they used to be active
  - Course progress stalled mid-module for 7+ days
- Rank by “winnability” not just risk — high-tenure members deserve
  more effort than month-1 members
- Cap the daily list at 5–7 names (creator can’t realistically DM 50
  people)

**Draft Message templates (creator picks tone)**

- **Sam style:** “sup — how’s everything going?”
- **Hamza style:** “saw you haven’t been around — what’s up bro,
  anything you need?”
- **Professional:** “Hey [name], noticed you’ve been quiet this week.
  Everything good? Let me know if there’s something I can help with.”

**Copy direction**

- Frame it as “DMs to send today” not “members at risk of churn”
  (operator action vs surveillance language)
- Show WHY each member was flagged in plain language (“hasn’t logged
  in for 2 weeks, used to post weekly”)
- Show the relationship history visible to Skool (“they’ve been a
  member 4 months, completed 60% of your course”)

**The “Draft Message” implementation (v1 vs v2)**

- **v1:** Copy to clipboard + open Skool DM tab. Honest disclosure:
  “Skool doesn’t allow us to send DMs directly yet. One click to copy,
  one click to paste in Skool.”
- **v2 (Chrome extension):** True 1-click send.

### Feature 5: Weekly Optimization Report

**What creator wisdom tells us**

- Creators run on intuition, not dashboards
- Weekly Cadence (one per week) > daily noise
- Sam reads through community every morning — owner’s mental model is
  qualitative
- “Show up daily and actually care” is the only consistent advice
- Pinned/featured language in the report itself signals what good
  looks like
- 30-day challenges spike engagement → recommendations should align
  with rituals

**Defaults & thresholds**

- Email lands Monday 7 AM creator’s **local time** (timezone
  auto-detected from browser at signup; editable in Settings)
- Length: 3–5 minute read max. No data dumps.
- Structure (ordered by what creator should DO this week, not what
  happened last week):
  1. One thing to celebrate (specific member win or community moment to
     amplify in a post)
  2. One person to DM today (highest-leverage check-in)
  3. One lesson to fix (drop-off pattern that emerged)
  4. One pattern worth knowing (engagement trend, time of day, etc.)
  5. One question for the creator (forces them to think, not just
     consume)
- First-week welcome variant: less data, more orientation. “We don’t
  have enough data yet to give you trends. Here’s what we’ll be
  tracking, and here’s what to do this week to seed it.”

**Copy direction (the AI voice)**

- Read like a thoughtful operator, not a SaaS report
- Reference specific member names and specific lessons (not “Member
  #4729”)
- Cite creator-wisdom directly when relevant: “Andrew Kirby found
  that drop-off in module 1 predicts 90-day churn. Your module 1
  lesson 3 has 42% drop-off — this is worth fixing this week.”
- Honest about uncertainty: “We don’t know WHY Sarah went quiet. The
  only way to find out is to ask her.”

**What we explicitly DON’T do**

- No “your community grew 12% this week” growth-marketing language
- No comparison to other communities
- No charts that don’t drive an action
- No more than 3 actions per week (overwhelm rule)

## Part 3 — Cross-Cutting Product Principles (the worldview)

Every feature should reinforce these. If a feature contradicts one,
redesign or kill it.

### 1. We help creators DO LESS, not more

The single most counterintuitive insight from this material: every
successful creator’s growth came from subtracting, not adding. AI Jack
deleted content and churn went 30% → 5.2%. Schoolers cut posting
frequency and retention rose. Sam: “Doing less, you’ll retain them
more.”

Every feature should make the creator’s life simpler. If we add a
notification, we earn it. If we suggest an action, we limit it to one.
If we show a metric, it has to drive a decision.

### 2. We strengthen relationships, not surveil members

The retention system is one-on-one DMs and meetups. Our product is an
operating system for that, not a churn-prevention dashboard. Frame
everything as “what to do for this person today” not “this member is
at risk.”

### 3. We trust creator intuition

Sam, Kirby, Hamza all run on feel — they read the community every
morning, they vibe-check, they don’t optimize numbers. We surface
signals; we don’t make decisions for the creator.

### 4. We respect Skool’s design intent

Skool’s whole product philosophy is “show up, build relationships,
have fun.” Our tool extends that philosophy, doesn’t fight it. We
never recommend tactics that turn community into a content-delivery
machine.

### 5. We’re transparent about what we can’t know

We can detect drop-off but not why. We can flag at-risk members but
can’t predict who’ll cancel. We say so. The Churn Reason Tracker
(Phase 2) addresses this honestly — log what departing members
actually said, build the why over time.

## Part 4 — Open Questions for Build Phase

Things creator wisdom didn’t fully resolve, that we should answer
during beta:

1. **Daily vs weekly cadence for Member Check-ins.** Wisdom says one
   weekly action; but a member sliding into inactivity may need
   same-day attention. Compromise: weekly digest + daily “urgent”
   alert if a high-tenure member crosses threshold.
2. **How aggressive should the “delete content” recommendation be?**
   Wisdom is clear that less is more, but creators are emotionally
   attached to content they made. Soft language v1, escalate based on
   data v2.
3. **First-week experience for empty communities.** A creator with 5
   members can’t get useful drop-off insights. What’s the value during
   weeks 1–4? Probably: relationship-building tools (intro post
   template, weekly call schedule, DM ladder reminders) — borrow the
   onboarding wisdom Sam/Kirby teach.
4. **Pricing tier alignment with Hormozi’s value equation.** Lower
   risk + faster results + less effort. Our $47 founding price hits
   “lower risk” hard. v2 messaging should emphasize “faster to your
   first 100 members” or similar concrete time-to-result claim.
